== Abstract

Kanecta is an open-source, MIT-licensed data storage and sharing protocol designed
to serve as a human-AI bridge. It treats every piece of information — whether a
paragraph, a decision, a person, a code snippet, or an entire document — as a
first-class object with a globally unique identifier, explicit type, and rich
semantic relationships.

This document captures the full vision behind Kanecta as developed through
extended conversations between Richard Thomas (the creator) and Claude (the
AI assistant). It is written so that another technically capable, AI-savvy
developer could pick up the project and continue it faithfully if Richard
were unavailable.

The document is intentionally long. Vision documents that get summarized
into bullet points lose the reasoning, the rejected alternatives, and the
human context that make decisions defensible later. Read it in full before
making architectural changes.

== How to Read This Document

This document is organized to be read either linearly (top to bottom) or by
jumping to specific sections. The intended reading order is:

. *Origins and Motivation* — Why this project exists at all
. *Core Philosophy* — The principles that should not be compromised
. *The Vision* — What Kanecta is and what it is not
. *Architecture and Specification* — How it works at the file-system level
. *Strategic Context* — The community group and first real-world deployment
. *AI Integration* — Why Kanecta is genuinely better than Obsidian for AI
  workflows
. *Roadmap* — The phased plan
. *Decision Log* — Why specific choices were made, with alternatives considered

If you only have time for one section, read *Core Philosophy*. Everything
else follows from it.

= Part I: Why Kanecta Exists

== Origins and Motivation

=== The Personal Itch

Richard has been thinking about this project for over two decades. He has
built at least four prior versions over the past four years alone. Each
attempt ran out of runway because the scope was too large for a single
developer working in spare time. The arrival of AI-assisted coding
(specifically Claude Code, GitHub Copilot, and similar tools) has finally
made the scope tractable.

The original itch is a familiar one to anyone who has accumulated digital
information over decades:

* Folders of pictures scattered across drives and services
* To-do lists abandoned when one app stopped working and another took over
* Notes in three different note-taking apps, each containing fragments of
  the truth
* Bookmarks, organizers, databases, web services — each promising to be the
  one place where everything lives, each eventually abandoned

Over the years, what should have been a steadily accumulating archive of
personal knowledge instead became fragmented, partially lost, and largely
inaccessible. Migration is painful; staying put while the tool stagnates
is worse.

=== The Broader Problem

This is not just a personal problem. Businesses, organizations, and
individuals all face the same pattern:

* Data gets locked into proprietary formats
* Software-as-a-Service vendors can change pricing, features, or simply
  shut down
* When you move, you lose context, relationships, and structure
* Even when you stay, you cannot easily query, link, or restructure your
  own information across the silos you have created

The semantic web (Tim Berners-Lee's vision) addressed something similar at
a global scale. Microsoft's WinFS — referenced by Bill Gates as one of his
biggest regrets about what didn't ship — was attempting to solve it at the
operating-system level. Neither shipped in a form that achieved adoption.
Kanecta is, in spirit, a much smaller and more practical attempt at the
same family of problems: treat information as structured, addressable,
relatable objects rather than opaque files.

=== Why Now

Three things have changed:

. *AI as a force multiplier.* What used to take a team of developers a year
  can now be built by one motivated person with AI assistance in weeks or
  months. The scope problem that killed prior attempts is now tractable.
. *Cognitive overload from AI itself.* Developers and knowledge workers are
  drowning in AI-generated context — Claude conversations, MCP servers,
  agent definitions, skills, prompts, generated artifacts. Existing tools
  (Obsidian, Notion, Jira) were not designed for this. There is a real,
  acute pain point right now.
. *Acceleration disrupts traditional planning.* In Richard's workplace, a
  developer using Claude Code can complete in two days what was planned to
  span three or four sprints. This breaks SAFe/agile planning processes
  and creates friction between developers and product owners. Capturing
  *why* decisions were made (not just *what* was decided) becomes
  essential institutional knowledge in this new environment.

== The Author's Honesty

Richard has been candid throughout the conversations that produced this
vision: he is not certain Kanecta has a clear product-market fit. He is
technically fascinated by the concept and has been since before AI made it
realistic. He would build it as a hobby regardless of commercial outcome.

This honesty matters because it shapes the project's culture:

* No investors are sought. There is no exit strategy. The license is MIT.
* Adoption is treated as a happy bonus, not a requirement.
* The focus is on doing the technical work *right* rather than rushing to
  market.
* If the project succeeds, it succeeds as a public good.

A successor maintainer should preserve this culture. The moment Kanecta
becomes "a startup," it loses what makes it different from the dozens of
prior knowledge-management products.

= Part II: Core Philosophy

== Principles That Should Not Be Compromised

These are the principles that have emerged from years of thinking about
this problem. A successor maintainer is welcome to question them — but
should do so with full awareness of what each one is trying to prevent.

=== Principle 1: MIT Open Source, Always

The license is MIT. This is deliberate.

MIT is the most widely understood permissive open-source license. The
reasoning:

* Anyone can use, modify, fork, and build on Kanecta without restriction.
  This maximises adoption and lowers the barrier for individuals,
  organisations, and businesses to participate.
* The data format remains open regardless of what is built on top of it.
  The protocol specification is published; alternative implementations
  are always possible.
* MIT is compatible with every other open-source license, so any
  dependency can be used without licence conflict concerns.

A consequence: Kanecta does not and cannot prevent commercial use. This
is a deliberate trade-off in favour of adoption and contributor freedom.

=== Principle 2: Data Longevity Over Convenience

The format must be readable in 50 years. This influences every decision:

* Text-based storage (JSON, markdown) over binary databases
* Files on disk over server-only databases
* Self-hostable over cloud-only
* Open formats over proprietary ones

When Richard says "I learned this lesson from Linux," he means it
literally. The Linux operating system he started using decades ago is
still usable today because it is open. The same must be true of Kanecta
data: a file written today should be openable by a human with a text
editor in 2076.

This is why Kanecta uses a file-system-as-database approach instead of
something like SQLite or PostgreSQL as the primary store. A SQLite file
written in 2026 will be readable in 2076 — probably. A folder of JSON
files will be readable in 2076 — certainly.

=== Principle 3: AI-Native, Not AI-Bolted-On

Kanecta is designed from the ground up to be manipulated by AI directly.

This is different from Obsidian, Notion, Roam, and most other knowledge
tools. Those tools were designed for humans and had AI features added
later. Kanecta is designed for AI to read, write, link, and reason over —
with a user interface added on top for humans.

Concrete consequences:

* Every item has a stable UUID. AI agents can reference items
  unambiguously across sessions.
* Every item has an explicit `type`. AI does not have to infer structure
  from prose.
* Relationships are typed (depends-on, blocks, enables, contradicts).
  AI can reason about dependencies, not just text proximity.
* Metadata is queryable. AI can ask "show me all decisions tagged
  security-related with confidence=decided" without parsing markdown.
* The protocol is filesystem-first. AI agents like Claude Code can read
  and write directly without needing an API layer in the way.

This is the *killer feature* for the current AI moment. It is also the
strongest argument against the (otherwise reasonable) suggestion to just
use Obsidian.

=== Principle 4: Human-AI Bridge

Despite being AI-native, Kanecta must remain transparent to humans.

* UUIDs are textual, not binary. A human can grep for them.
* Files are on disk, in directories, in plain text. A human can browse
  them.
* Relationships are stored as JSON, not opaque pointers. A human can
  audit them.
* The history folder lets a human see how items have changed over time.

This is the *human-AI bridge*: the AI gets structured data it can reason
about efficiently; the human gets a system they can verify and trust.
Most AI tools hide the reasoning. Kanecta makes it visible and
traceable.

=== Principle 5: Distributed by Design

Kanecta is not a server you connect to. It is a protocol that runs
locally on each user's machine. Multiple Kanecta instances can connect
peer-to-peer, share items, subscribe to each other's updates.

This is critical for:

* *Privacy*: Sensitive personal or organizational data never has to
  leave a user's machine to be useful.
* *Resilience*: There is no central server to take down. If GitHub
  disappears, Kanecta still works.
* *Trust*: Each user owns their data and chooses what to share. The
  `owner` field on every item makes provenance explicit.
* *Scaling*: A team of 130 developers does not require central
  infrastructure. Each runs their own instance and connects to
  colleagues' instances directly.

=== Principle 6: Eat Your Own Dog Food, Immediately

The first real use of Kanecta should be managing Kanecta's own
development. The plan, the spec, the decisions, the to-do list — all
stored in Kanecta from day one.

This is not just a clever marketing line. It is the fastest way to
discover what works and what doesn't. If the protocol cannot organize
its own development effectively, it will not work for anyone else.

=== Principle 7: Lean Scope, Iterative Expansion

Richard has tried to build the full vision four times. Each attempt
failed because the scope was too large. The lesson learned: ship the
smallest thing that is useful, then expand.

Concrete rule: every feature added must be justified by a real, current
use case. Speculative features ("we might need X later") are added to
the roadmap, not the codebase.

= Part III: The Vision

== What Kanecta Is

In one sentence:

____
Kanecta is a protocol for storing structured, addressable, relatable
information locally on disk, designed for AI agents to read and write
directly while remaining auditable by humans.
____

In a paragraph:

Kanecta treats every piece of information — a paragraph, a person, a
decision, an image, a code snippet, an annotation — as a first-class
object with a UUID, an explicit type, ownership, timestamps, optional
tags, and typed relationships to other objects. These objects live as
JSON files on disk in a sharded directory structure that scales from
hundreds of items to millions without changing the addressing scheme.
On top of this protocol, multiple applications can be built: knowledge
managers, community websites, AI workflow tools, task trackers,
document editors, governance systems, and more.

== What Kanecta Is Not

It is important to be precise about what Kanecta is *not*, because the
adjacent space is crowded with tools that look superficially similar.

* *Kanecta is not Obsidian.* Obsidian stores markdown files for humans
  to read. Kanecta stores structured items for AI to query and humans
  to audit. The text-first vs. structure-first distinction is
  fundamental.
* *Kanecta is not Notion.* Notion is a hosted SaaS with proprietary
  data formats. Kanecta is self-hosted with open formats.
* *Kanecta is not a database.* Databases are tuned for transactional
  workloads. Kanecta is tuned for human and AI authoring, with eventual
  consistency between peers.
* *Kanecta is not a wiki.* Wikis store pages. Kanecta stores arbitrarily
  fine-grained items — a single paragraph or even a single bullet point
  can be its own addressable item.
* *Kanecta is not a knowledge graph database.* Graph databases optimize
  for query performance. Kanecta optimizes for human readability and
  durability.
* *Kanecta is not WordPress.* This was considered as the backing store
  for the first real-world application (Featherston Community Hub) and
  rejected. The reasoning is documented later.
* *Kanecta is not a competitor to Claude or any AI model.* It is a
  *complement* — a place where AI-generated context can be captured
  and accumulated over time.

== The Killer Features

If a successor needs to pitch Kanecta to a skeptical audience (developers,
community groups, organizations), these are the features that matter:

. *Every paragraph has a UUID.* You can link to a specific bullet point
  from anywhere, in any document or application.
. *Typed relationships.* Not just "this links to that" but "this depends on
  that, blocks that, contradicts that, supersedes that." AI can reason
  about dependencies.
. *Decision logs with reasoning.* Capture not just *what* was decided but
  *why*, with alternatives considered. Compounds in value over time.
. *Token efficiency for AI users.* Pull only the relevant context from
  Kanecta into a Claude conversation, instead of accumulating long chat
  histories. Cheaper, faster, smarter.
. *MIT guarantee.* Your data is yours forever. The protocol is open;
  anyone can build a compatible implementation.
. *Peer-to-peer team aggregation.* Multiple developers' Kanecta stores
  can sync with each other, building team-wide institutional memory
  without central infrastructure.
. *Self-hostable, no cloud required.* Run it on a laptop. Run it on a
  VPS. Run it on a Raspberry Pi. Your choice.

= Part IV: Architecture and Specification

== The 30-Second Architecture

Each user has a *datastore* — a folder on their disk containing a hidden
`.kanecta/` directory with everything Kanecta needs. Inside that:

* `data/` is the source of truth — every item as a JSON file in a sharded
  folder structure.
* Several index caches (`types/`, `tags/`, `links/`, `relationships/`,
  `search/`, `remotes-index/`) speed up common queries.
* `aliases/` lets humans give memorable names to items.
* `annotations/` lets people comment on items without modifying them.
* `history/` preserves snapshots when items change.
* `remotes/` caches items owned by other users you have synced with.
* `config/` holds your owner identity and other settings.

Items reference each other in two ways: by embedding `[[uuid]]` syntax in
text values, or via typed relationships stored separately. A search index
makes the whole datastore queryable.

That is the entire architecture. Everything else is detail.

== Sharding Strategy: 2 + 2 + Full UUID

This deserves its own section because it has been the subject of careful
thought.

=== The Problem

If you put one million items in a single directory, the filesystem will
slow to a crawl. If you put them in a deeply nested tree, traversal
becomes expensive. The sharding strategy must balance both.

=== The Solution

Kanecta uses UUID version 4 (random) for all item identifiers. UUID v4
gives 122 bits of randomness — collisions are statistically impossible
without a central registry.

For sharding, take the UUID (with hyphens stripped for the shard
computation), take the first 2 characters and the next 2 characters as
two directory levels, then use the *full UUID* (with hyphens preserved)
as the third level.

Example:

* UUID: `a1b2c3d4-e5f6-4abc-9def-123456789012`
* First 2 chars: `a1`
* Next 2 chars: `b2`
* Full path: `.kanecta/data/a1/b2/a1b2c3d4-e5f6-4abc-9def-123456789012/`

=== Why This Works

* *Distribution*: 256 × 256 = 65,536 possible shard combinations. Even
  with millions of items, no single shard directory becomes overwhelmed.
* *Recoverability*: The full UUID appears in the path. If the index
  caches are lost or corrupted, the data can be reconstructed by walking
  the tree.
* *Performance*: A direct lookup by UUID computes its path in constant
  time. No tree traversal needed.
* *Consistency*: The same sharding pattern is used everywhere — `data/`,
  `types/`, `links/`, `relationships/`, `annotations/`, `history/`,
  `tags/`, and (with string-based sharding) `aliases/` and
  `remotes-index/`.

=== Why Not Other Approaches

Alternatives that were considered and rejected:

* *2-char-only sharding throughout the UUID* (16 levels deep): too much
  filesystem nesting, slow traversal.
* *3-char-only sharding* (4096 dirs per level): overkill for any
  realistic scale; less human-readable.
* *No sharding* (flat directory of UUIDs): falls over at scale on most
  filesystems.
* *Sharding by content hash*: defeats the purpose; the UUID is already
  random and unique.
* *Sharding by 2 + 2 + remainder (UUID minus first 4 chars)*: rejected
  because losing the full UUID in the path makes recovery harder.

== The Item Schema

Every item is a JSON file with the following fields. Required fields are
marked.

[cols="1,1,3"]
|===
| Field | Required | Description

| `id`
| yes
| UUID v4 of this item

| `parentId`
| yes (nullable)
| UUID of parent item, or null for root-level items

| `value`
| no
| The item's content — text, a UUID for symlinks, or null

| `type`
| yes
| Primitive (`string`, `number`, `text`, `file`, `symlink`) or structured
  (`object`, `decision`, `annotation`)

| `typeId`
| conditional
| For `object` items, UUID of the type definition. Otherwise null.

| `owner`
| yes
| Email or domain identifying ownership

| `license`
| no
| License identifier (MIT, Apache-2.0, CC-BY, etc.)

| `sortOrder`
| yes
| Integer for ordering among siblings

| `confidence`
| no
| `experimental`, `exploring`, `decided`, or `locked`

| `tags`
| no
| Array of cross-cutting tag strings

| `createdAt`
| yes
| ISO8601 timestamp

| `modifiedAt`
| yes
| ISO8601 timestamp

| `createdBy`
| yes
| Email or domain of creator

| `modifiedBy`
| yes
| Email or domain of last modifier

| `cachedAt`
| conditional
| For items in `remotes/`, when they were last fetched

| `subscribedAt`
| no
| When a subscription to this remote item began

| `subscriptionSource`
| no
| URL or identifier of the remote source

|===

== Types: Primitive and Object

=== Primitive Types

* `string` — Short textual value
* `number` — Numeric value
* `text` — Longer text content
* `file` — Item content is a file in the item's folder
* `symlink` — `value` contains a UUID pointing to another item

=== Object Types

Object types are user- or community-defined schemas. The `type` field is
`object` and the `typeId` field references another item — a type
definition — that describes the expected structure.

For example, a "Person" object type might define fields for name, email,
phone, and notes. Any item of that type is expected (though not strictly
enforced by the filesystem) to follow the schema.

=== Built-in Structured Types

* `decision` — A decision log entry. The `value` field contains a JSON
  object capturing the decision, the problem, alternatives considered,
  trade-offs, reasoning, decider, and timestamp.
* `annotation` — A comment on another item. Stored in `annotations/`
  rather than `data/`.

== Indexes and Caches

The following folders inside `.kanecta/` are *derived* from `data/`,
`history/`, `annotations/`, `aliases/`, `remotes/`, and `config/`. They
can be rebuilt at any time:

* `types/` — Maps each `typeId` to the items of that type
* `tags/` — Maps each tag string to the items carrying that tag
* `links/` — For each item, the items that link *to* it (backlinks)
* `relationships/` — Typed semantic relationships (depends-on, blocks,
  etc.) in both directions
* `search/` — Full-text search index (managed by a search library)
* `remotes-index/` — Maps each remote owner to the items of theirs you
  have cached

When a successor maintainer adds a new query pattern, the right answer is
usually to add another index folder rather than scanning `data/`.

== Multi-User and Remote Items

A user's `.kanecta/data/` contains only items they own. Items owned by
other users that they have cached locally live in `.kanecta/remotes/`,
with the same sharding pattern.

`.kanecta/remotes-index/` provides fast lookup by owner: "give me all
items I have cached from `phil@gmail.com`."

Subscription is the act of asking a remote owner's Kanecta instance to
notify you when their items change. The `subscriptionSource` field
records where to fetch updates from.

For very large datastores, an append-only `changelog.jsonl` (planned
extension) records every create/update/delete so peers can sync
efficiently without rescanning the whole filesystem.

== Performance Notes

Several performance questions came up during design and are worth
recording.

=== File-System Read Performance

For typical AI workflows, the filesystem is far faster than the AI
itself. Reading a JSON metadata file on a modern SSD takes 1-5
milliseconds. Even with hundreds of items per session, total I/O is
trivial compared to token processing.

A RAM cache is *not* needed for the MVP. Add it only if profiling shows
filesystem reads becoming a bottleneck, which is unlikely until you
reach hundreds of thousands of items being queried frequently.

=== Type Index Scale

If a single type has millions of items, a single `items.json` file
becomes unwieldy. The solution is to shard the type index in the same
2 + 2 pattern — store items in `.kanecta/types/[type-uuid]/aa/bb/items.json`
where `aa/bb` is the shard of the contained item's UUID. This is a
future optimization, not needed for MVP scale.

=== Backup Strategy

* Small stores (< 10k items): commit `.kanecta/` to git. Simple,
  versioned, easy.
* Medium stores (10k - 100k items): use rsync with `--delete` for
  incremental backups.
* Large stores (millions of items): the planned `changelog.jsonl`
  enables backup tools to read only the changes since last backup,
  avoiding a full filesystem scan.

Index caches (`types/`, `tags/`, `links/`, etc.) do not strictly need
to be backed up — they can be rebuilt from `data/`. This is an
important property to preserve.

= Part V: Strategic Context

== The Featherston Community Hub: First Real Use

The first real-world application built on Kanecta is *Community Hub*, a
platform deployed at https://featherston.co.nz for a small community in
New Zealand.

=== What Community Hub Does

* *Public website* for the community
* *Real-time threaded discussions* (Slack-like) for community members
* Dedicated sections for food, local produce, skill sharing, transport,
  social services, local economy, and community resilience planning
* *Financial management*: transactions, cashflow, P&L, recurring
  expenses, categorized income/expenditure
* *Governance*: constitution, bylaws, board procedures, AGM workflows,
  content moderation, volunteer onboarding, complaint handling
* *Role-based access* via Keycloak SSO
* *Push notifications* and a fully responsive mobile layout

=== Current State

As of the time of this document, Community Hub is deployed and in use.
The technology stack is React/TypeScript on the frontend, Node.js /
Express on the backend, PostgreSQL for data (not yet using Kanecta as
the backing store), and Keycloak for authentication.

=== Why Not WordPress

The community group could, in principle, use WordPress. This was
considered carefully and rejected. The reasoning:

* WordPress plugin ecosystem leads to a janky Frankenstein system
  that requires technical maintenance the community group cannot
  provide.
* WordPress is GPL but the plugin ecosystem pushes hard toward paid
  plugins; the community group does not want paid software.
* The VPS that hosts Keycloak and Nginx has 2GB of RAM; adding
  WordPress, MySQL, and PHP-FPM is tight.
* Keycloak + OAuth integration with WordPress is not native and
  requires custom plugins.
* The custom React app is *already deployed* and meets their needs;
  switching to WordPress now would be a regression.

The honest takeaway: the lock-in concern is not really about
technology choice. It is about having technical people willing to
maintain whatever is built. The path forward is to make the React app
open-source, well-documented, and easy for other developers to
contribute to — which is exactly what is happening.

=== The Page Editor Gap

The one feature Community Hub does not yet have, which the community
group most needs, is the ability for non-technical users to create and
edit public pages without involving Richard. Adding a WYSIWYG editor
(TipTap, Slate, or similar — all open-source) and storing pages as
Kanecta items closes this gap.


== Peer-to-Peer Sync Across a Network

Real-time team aggregation works by having each developer run a local
Kanecta server and sync with colleagues over HTTPS on port 443. Each
instance is reachable directly by its hostname or IP address.

This approach requires no shared infrastructure: no shared drives, no
central server, no IT provisioning. Each developer owns their store
and chooses what to share with whom.

The sensitive-data concern is real and must be handled. Sanitization
rules should be built into the sync layer so that items tagged
`sensitive` or items with certain `license` values are not shared
without explicit opt-in.

= Part VI: AI Integration

This part is the most important section for a successor maintainer to
understand, because it is where Kanecta differs most clearly from its
alternatives.

== Why Kanecta Beats Obsidian for AI Workflows

Obsidian is a beautiful tool for human note-taking. It stores markdown
files in folders, links them with `[[note-name]]` syntax, and renders
them with a clean UI. For humans writing notes, it is excellent.

For AI workflows, it is suboptimal. The reasons:

* Markdown is a *human* format. AI must parse natural language to
  understand structure. Kanecta gives AI structured types directly.
* Obsidian links are by note name. Renaming a note can break links.
  Kanecta links are by UUID and are stable forever.
* Obsidian has no concept of typed relationships. "Note A links to
  note B" is the only relationship type. Kanecta has depends-on,
  blocks, enables, supersedes, contradicts, etc.
* Obsidian has no concept of confidence levels, decision logs, or
  reasoning capture. Kanecta does.
* Obsidian conflates the document with the note. In Kanecta, a single
  paragraph or bullet point can be its own addressable item.

The deeper point: Obsidian was designed before LLMs existed. Kanecta is
designed *for* LLM-augmented workflows.

== What Claude Wants (Asked Directly)

When asked what an AI would want from a local data store, the answer
was:

. *Explicit structure.* Not "here's markdown, infer the meaning." Every
  piece of data has explicit type information.
. *Rich, typed linking.* Not just text references. Semantic
  relationships that can be queried.
. *Flat addressability.* UUID-per-item means anything can be referenced
  directly. No need to know which file contains a particular
  paragraph.
. *Queryability.* "Show me all decisions about authentication" should
  be a fast structured query, not a regex search.
. *Metadata as first-class.* Provenance, ownership, timestamps,
  confidence — all queryable.
. *Multi-user native.* Sharing and subscriptions baked in, not bolted
  on.
. *Decision logs with reasoning.* Capture *why*, not just *what*. So
  that when re-visiting decisions months later, the original reasoning
  is available.
. *Relationship semantics.* Knowing that A *blocks* B is more useful
  than just knowing A and B are related.

Kanecta now provides all of these.

== Token Efficiency

A subtle but valuable benefit: Kanecta enables *token-efficient* AI
workflows.

The naive approach to using Claude (or any LLM) over long conversations
is to let the chat history grow. Every new message pays for the cost
of re-sending the entire history. After hours of conversation, the
token cost per response is substantial.

With Kanecta:

* Important decisions and outputs from each conversation are saved to
  the local store.
* New conversations start fresh, but pull *only relevant context* from
  Kanecta on demand.
* "Tell me what we decided about authentication for Product A" pulls
  the relevant decision items (perhaps 5KB) instead of replaying the
  full conversation history (perhaps 50KB).

Over months of use, this compounds into real cost savings. For
professional developers on Claude Pro, this matters.

== The Capture Strategies

Several approaches to getting AI-generated content into Kanecta were
considered. They differ in friction and completeness.

=== Strategy 1: Manual Slash Command

User types `/kanecta save-conversation` in their Claude interface,
which triggers a save. Low infrastructure, requires user action each
time. *Recommended for MVP.*

=== Strategy 2: `~/.claude/` Configuration Hook

Add a snippet to the user's local Claude configuration folder that
tells Claude about Kanecta and instructs it to offer saving naturally
during conversations. Works for Claude Code and CLI; does *not* work
for the Claude web app or mobile apps (these do not read the local
config). *Recommended for the connector npm package.*

=== Strategy 3: MCP Server

Build a Model Context Protocol server that exposes Kanecta operations
(create item, link items, query, etc.) as tools that Claude Code can
call directly. Most seamless integration. *Recommended for v2.*

=== Strategy 4: Browser Extension

For users who work primarily in the Claude web app, a browser
extension can capture conversations and post them to the local Kanecta
server. *Future work.*

=== Strategy 5: Watcher Script

A small Node.js daemon watches a temp folder for JSON files. Claude (or
the user) writes session data to that folder; the watcher imports it
into Kanecta following the spec's business rules. *Useful for batch
import scenarios.*

= Part VIII: Decision Log

These are major decisions made during design, with the alternatives
considered and reasons for rejection. A successor maintainer should
update this log when making significant changes.

== Decision: UUID v4 over Other UUID Versions

Decided: 2026-05-14. Confidence: locked.

UUID v4 (random) chosen over v1 (timestamp-based), v6/v7 (time-ordered),
and v5 (namespace-based).

Reasoning: v4 has no central authority requirement, no information
leakage (v1 includes MAC address), and is universally supported. The
122 bits of randomness make collisions effectively impossible.

Alternatives considered:

* *UUID v1*: leaks MAC address; not acceptable for distributed,
  privacy-focused systems.
* *UUID v6/v7*: time-ordered, allows efficient indexing by creation
  time. Tempting, but added complexity and reduced library support.
  Revisit if time-ordering becomes a critical query pattern.
* *Snowflake IDs*: require a coordinator. Rejected on principle.

== Decision: 2 + 2 + Full UUID Sharding

Decided: 2026-05-14. Confidence: locked.

The shard path is: `[first 2 chars]/[next 2 chars]/[full UUID with hyphens]/`.

Reasoning: Balances filesystem performance (no directory has more than
a few thousand items at reasonable scale) with recoverability (the full
UUID is visible in the path, so if index caches are lost, data can be
walked and reconstructed).

Alternatives considered and rejected:

* *2-char-only deep nesting (16 levels)*: too slow for traversal.
* *3-char-only sharding*: overkill, less human-readable.
* *No sharding*: fails at scale.
* *2 + 2 + remainder*: loses recoverability.

== Decision: MIT License

Decided: 2026-05-14. Confidence: locked.

MIT chosen for its simplicity, wide recognition, and maximum
permissiveness.

Reasoning: MIT is the most widely understood open-source license. It
places no restrictions on how Kanecta can be used, forked, or built
upon, which maximises adoption potential and removes friction for
contributors. The protocol specification is published openly regardless
— users can always build compatible implementations.

Alternatives considered:

* *AGPL*: Would require SaaS providers to share modifications, but
  significantly raises the adoption barrier and complicates dependency
  selection. Rejected in favour of permissiveness.
* *GPL*: Copyleft without the SaaS provision. Rejected for the same
  reasons as AGPL.
* *Apache 2.0*: A reasonable alternative with an explicit patent grant.
  Could be revisited if patent concerns arise; MIT is preferred for its
  simplicity.

== Decision: PostgreSQL Backing Store for Community Hub (Temporary)

Decided: pre-2026. Confidence: decided (not locked).

Community Hub currently uses PostgreSQL, not Kanecta, as its backing
store.

Reasoning: PostgreSQL was already running on the VPS for Keycloak.
Adding Community Hub to the existing database was pragmatic. Migrating
Community Hub to use Kanecta as the backing store is planned for a
future phase, once Kanecta is mature enough.

This is a *transitional* decision and should not become permanent. A
successor maintainer should plan to migrate Community Hub to Kanecta
when the protocol can meet its performance and feature needs.

== Decision: No WordPress for Community Hub

Decided: 2026-05-14. Confidence: decided.

WordPress was considered as an alternative to building a custom React
app. Rejected for the reasons documented in Part V.

Note: this decision is somewhat reversible. If maintaining the custom
React app becomes infeasible, WordPress with carefully chosen plugins
remains a fallback option.


== Decision: P2P HTTPS on Port 443 for Team Sync

Decided: 2026-05-14. Confidence: experimental.

Each developer runs a local Kanecta server reachable by peers over
HTTPS on port 443. Peers query each other directly; no shared
central server is required.

Alternatives considered:

* *Shared network drive*: Requires provisioning shared storage. Friction.
* *Sync daemon writing to a shared git repo*: Works but requires
  sanitization to avoid sensitive data leaks into version history.
* *Bastion host*: Mediates peer connections; more infrastructure but
  cleaner access control.

The P2P approach is chosen for its simplicity and zero-infrastructure
requirement. If direct peer connections are impractical in a given
network environment, fall back to git-based sync or a shared bastion.

== Decision: Markdown Spec, Not Self-Hosted in Kanecta (Yet)

Decided: 2026-05-14. Confidence: decided.

The specification itself is maintained as markdown files in the GitHub
repo, not as Kanecta items inside a Kanecta datastore.

Reasoning: chicken-and-egg. The spec is needed before the protocol can
store the spec. Once the protocol is stable, migrating the spec into a
Kanecta datastore is a worthwhile dog-fooding exercise.

== Decision: Embedded vs. Server Database

Decided: 2026-05-14. Confidence: decided.

For local Kanecta deployments, the filesystem is the database. No
SQLite, no embedded Postgres.

Reasoning: filesystems are more durable than database formats. A folder
of JSON files written in 2026 will be readable in 2076. A SQLite
database might be — but the longevity guarantee is weaker.

For cases that require fast queries (Community Hub running on
PostgreSQL is the current example), use a separate database alongside
Kanecta, with Kanecta as the source of truth and the database as a
query cache.

= Part IX: For the Successor Maintainer

If you are reading this because Richard is no longer leading the
project, welcome. Here is what you most need to know.

== What Not to Change

* The MIT license
* The filesystem-as-database approach for local datastores
* The UUID v4 + 2 + 2 + full sharding
* The principle that index caches are derived, not authoritative
* The eat-your-own-dog-food practice

== What You Are Free to Change

* The choice of any specific library (search, web framework, UI
  framework) — as long as the replacement is open-source
* The specific set of standard relationship types
* The list of confidence levels
* The exact roadmap phasing — some phases may be reordered, combined,
  or skipped based on real-world needs
* The first-class apps built on Kanecta — Community Hub is one
  example, others can and should follow

== What You Should Question Carefully

* The peer-to-peer sync approach if a clear better alternative emerges
  (e.g., a mature CRDT-based protocol)
* The single-owner model for items if multi-author editing becomes
  important
* The lack of strong type enforcement for object types

== How to Make Major Decisions

When facing a significant design choice:

. Read the relevant section of this document and the decision log.
. If the question is not answered, write down the question, the
  alternatives, and the reasoning *before* making the decision.
. Add the decision to the decision log with confidence level.
. Bias toward reversible decisions when possible.
. When in doubt, choose the option that preserves data longevity and
  user control.
