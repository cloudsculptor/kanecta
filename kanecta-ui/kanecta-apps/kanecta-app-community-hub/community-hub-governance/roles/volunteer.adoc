= Volunteer
:doctype: article

Volunteers are not helpers or support staff — they *are* the Society. This is the most important thing to understand about how Featherston Inc works. The Custodian Board oversees; volunteers do.

Every positive decision about the website, the community activities, and the Society's work belongs to volunteers. No approval from the Custodian Board is required. Volunteers self-organise, set their own priorities, and make their own operational decisions collectively.

== What volunteers do

Volunteers collectively own and run all of the Society's operational work:

* Writing, editing, and publishing content on the website;
* Building and maintaining the website's features and infrastructure;
* Organising community activities and events;
* Responding to member enquiries and contributions;
* Defining volunteer roles and recruiting new contributors; and
* Any other work that serves the Society's purpose of providing reliable, open community information for Featherston.

== How volunteers organise

Volunteers self-organise. There is no manager, no approval chain, and no hierarchy. Where volunteers disagree on an operational matter, they resolve it among themselves by discussion and consensus. The Custodian Board does not adjudicate operational disagreements unless a formal complaint is lodged under the Society's disputes process.

Volunteer roles are defined and published by volunteers themselves, openly and collaboratively. Any Voting Member may propose a new volunteer role or take on existing responsibilities.

== Becoming a volunteer

Any Voting Member may volunteer. There is no application process beyond expressing availability and agreeing to work in accordance with the Society's kaupapa and code of conduct.

Volunteers do not need to be paid members — they need only hold Voting Membership (a contribution of NZ$5 or more in the past 12 months) and be willing to contribute their time and skills.

== Trust and accountability

Volunteers are trusted to act in accordance with the Society's kaupapa. They are not directed or micromanaged — they are trusted to perform the work they have taken on.

The Custodian Board may receive formal complaints about volunteer conduct. If a volunteer is found to have materially breached the kaupapa or code of conduct, the Board may dismiss that volunteer after giving them a fair opportunity to respond. This is the Board's primary disciplinary tool and is not used lightly.

== Why this model?

Most organisations treat volunteers as auxiliary helpers who assist the paid staff or elected committee. This Society inverts that model entirely: there are no paid staff, and the elected committee (the Custodian Board) exists only to protect against serious misconduct — not to manage the work.

This design keeps the energy of the Society where it belongs: in the hands of the people who show up and do the work. It prevents the common failure mode where a small governing committee gradually absorbs all decision-making authority and volunteers become passive executors of top-down direction.
